Levels 101

This is a page for explaining basic levels concepts and answer frequently asked questions

Levels is commonly misunderstood—but there is no shame in that!

It is difficult for a first time user to grasp and there’s always more to learn for seasoned user too

Below you will see a few graphics and descriptions to help you visualize the foundational concepts in levels

Basics

An Introduction

Levels uses height values. The values consist of a bottom value and a top value, representing the lowest and highest height an actor, tile, wall, etc. occupy.

For this page, we will be using a theoretical set of 3 tiles. They will be represented by coloured squares. The graphics are to visualize their relationships with each other based on their height values.

The lowest layer will have 0 as its lowest height and 9 as its highest height. It is represented by [blue].

The medium layer will have 10 as its lowest height and 19 as its highest height. It is represented by [red].

The highest layer will have 20 as its lowest height and 29 as its highest height. It is represented by [yellow].

Stairs

Stairs allow a token to travel between elevations.

  • Stairs can only move tokens to its set elevations
  • The token must be on one of the two elevations exactly (e.g. 0 or 9) to trigger
  • Stairs will move tokens one value higher than its top elevation if entering from below

To better understand this, we will have a stair with the values of 0 and 9.

The solid lines represent the stair’s height in addition to its effective height due to Property #3 above.

Holes

A Hole will cut through the 3D space to allow vision to pass through tile polygons

  • Holes do not allow tokens to fall through—They are used for vision, not for movement.
  • Holes do not add transparency to the images in your tiles. You will not be able to see through them if there is not transparency in the area of the image the hole is over.

While setting up a hole, the best way to figure out the top and bottom values is to imagine standing inside the hole, look up, look down—the elevations your eyes would hit are the top and bottom values you need

Roofs

A roof is a tile intended to be the top of the building.

Under most roof settings, a roof is visible from afar but not visible while below it. There are many settings that allow a roof to be visible through fog, at a greater distance, or partially visible while underneath.

Roofs have many settings and opportunities to do unique things with. However, because there are many potential uses for roofs, roofs tend to cause quite a bit of confusion for people. This will only be a basic visual representation of roofs but a more in-depth page may be coming soon.

The values for a roof should be:

Bottom: The highest layer + 1
Top: Infinity

Notes

  • Each tile should represent only one range of elevations

For example: A tile that has both parts of a floor and a roof piece should be split into two tiles to avoid issues

  • The advanced mode is under the Better Roofs module settings

Please have this enabled when asking for help or troubleshooting
